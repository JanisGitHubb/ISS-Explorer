The only research laboratory in orbit.

The International Space Station (ISS) is a solar powered research laboratory orbiting Earth, representing a pinnacle of international collaboration in order to enhance our knowledge of space exploration. Launched in 1998, the ISS serves as a microgravity environment where scientists conduct groundbreaking experiments across various scientific disciplines.

It stands as the only continuously inhabited research facility in orbit, fostering a diverse array of studies in:

  • Physics
  • Biology
  • Astronomy
  • More

How was it built?

The assembly of the ISS, commencing in 1998 with the launch of the Russian module Zarya, was a meticulously planned effort spanning multiple spaceflights. Components were delivered by Russian Soyuz and Proton rockets, American Space Shuttles, and later, commercial spacecraft.

Regions responsible for the assembly:

  • Europe
  • United States
  • Canada
  • Japan
  • Russia

Astronauts conducted spacewalks to connect modules, install components, and deploy crucial solar arrays. This intricate process, symbolizing international cooperation, continued until 2011 when the ISS reached completion.

Why was it built?

The ISS was constructed with a dual purpose:

  • Scientific exploration
  • International cooperation

The ISS facilitates research across many fields, offering a unique environment not replicable on Earth. It was created to serve as a platform to study the effects of microgravity on living organisms, develop advanced materials, and conduct experiments with applications ranging from medicine to technology.

Beyond scientific endeavors, the ISS represents a pinnacle of global collaboration, involving space agencies from many different countries fostering peaceful partnerships in space exploration and advancing our understanding of the cosmos.

What has it accomplished?

The construction and operation of the ISS have yielded numerous accomplishments:

Advanced our understanding of microgravity effects on human health, leading to insights for long-duration space travel. Developed new materials with improved properties through experiments conducted in the unique environment of the ISS. It serves as an observatory for astronomical research.

Furthermore, international collaboration on the ISS has strengthened diplomatic ties and fostered peaceful cooperation in space exploration. Technologies developed for the ISS have also found applications on Earth, ranging from water purification systems to medical advancements.

Research

What's going on there?

Biological Research

The ISS plays a crucial role in understanding how space travel affects living organisms. Experiments are conducted to explore the impact of microgravity on the human body, studying bone density loss, muscle atrophy, and changes in the cardiovascular and immune systems. Additionally, the station serves as a unique environment for investigating plant growth, microbial behavior, and the effects of space conditions on small animals. Findings in biological research have implications for human health during extended space missions and offer vluable information about biological processes.

Material Sciences

The microgravity conditions aboard the ISS provide an ideal setting for studying the properties and behaviours of materials in ways that are not possible on Earth. Researchers investigate the synthesis of new materials, the formation of various alloys, and the behavior of fluids in the microgravity environment. Experiments carried out on the ISS contribute to big advancements in material sciences, leading to the development of stronger, lighter, and more durable materials with applications in aerospace, car engineering industry, architecture and medical technology.

Physics

Physicists leverage the microgravity environment of the ISS to conduct experiments that probe fundamental physical principles. Studies in fluid dynamics, combustion, and the behavior of fundamental forces offer insights into the laws governing physical phenomena. The results of these experiments not only contribute to scientific knowledge but also have practical applications, influencing the design and improvement of technologies on Earth.

Astronomy and Astrophysics

The ISS serves as a unique vantage point for astronomers and astrophysicists to observe the universe. Free from the distortion caused by Earth's atmosphere, the station allows researchers to study distant galaxies, stars, and cosmic phenomena with exceptional clarity. This observational data contributes to a deeper understanding of the cosmos, aiding in the exploration of dark matter, the formation of galaxies, and the nature of black holes.

Psychology

Living and working in space can be challenging both mentally and emotionally due to factors such as isolation, confinement, altered sleep patterns, and the stress of performing complex tasks in a unique environment. Therefore, studying these aspects is crucial for ensuring the well-being and performance of astronauts during long-duration missions, such as those planned for future deep-space exploration. Researchers on the ISS conduct experiments to study various aspects of human behavior, cognition, and performance in space. This includes studies on sleep quality, cognitive function, mood changes, and stress management techniques.

Fluid Dynamics

Fluid dynamics research in microgravity provides valuable insights into how fluids behave in the absence of gravity, which can differ significantly from their behavior on Earth. Understanding fluid dynamics in space is crucial for various applications, including spacecraft propulsion, life support systems, and manufacturing processes. Researchers conduct experiments aboard the ISS to study phenomena such as capillary flow, surface tension-driven flows, and multiphase flow behavior in microgravity conditions. These experiments often involve specialized equipment and techniques to control and manipulate fluids in the unique environment of space.

Andreas Mogensen

Role: As a flight engineer on the ISS, Andreas Mogensen plays a crucial part in maintaining the station’s systems, conducting scientific experiments, and ensuring the safety of the crew. Interesting Fact: Before becoming an astronaut, Mogensen was an aerospace engineer and a fighter pilot. During his mission, he remotely operated rovers on Earth, showcasing his versatility and adaptability in space exploration.

Jasmin Moghbeli

Role: Jasmin Moghbeli serves as the mission commander for SpaceX Crew-7, responsible for overseeing the crew’s activities and ensuring mission success. As a flight engineer during Expedition 69/70, she contributes to scientific research and station maintenance. Interesting Fact: Moghbeli’s journey to space began as a U.S. Marine Corps test pilot, where she honed her skills in high-performance aircraft. Her Houston roots and determination make her an inspiring figure in the world of space exploration.

Satoshi Furukawa

Role: Satoshi Furukawa has been a flight engineer on two long-duration missions: Expedition 28/29 (2011) and Expedition 69/70 (2023-2024). His responsibilities include conducting experiments, performing spacewalks, and maintaining the ISS. Interesting Fact: Furukawa’s diverse background includes being a medical doctor, engineer, and astronaut. He brings a unique perspective to space research and has contributed significantly to our understanding of microgravity effects on the human body.

Loral O'Hara

Role: Loral O’Hara, as a flight engineer for Expedition 70, collaborates with her crewmates to keep the ISS operational. She conducts experiments, repairs equipment, and ensures the station’s sustainability. Interesting Fact: O’Hara’s passion for exploration extends beyond space—she is an accomplished scuba diver, pilot, and geologist. Her multidisciplinary expertise enriches the scientific endeavors aboard the ISS.

Konstantin Borisov

Role: As a flight engineer on Expedition 69/70, Konstantin Borisov contributes to station maintenance, scientific research, and crew well-being. His expertise in engineering and space operations is invaluable. Interesting Fact: Borisov’s journey began as a Russian Air Force pilot, and he later transitioned to cosmonaut training. His participation in the SpaceX Crew-7 mission demonstrates international collaboration in space exploration.

Oleg Kononenko

Role: Oleg Kononenko, the commander of Expedition 70, oversees the crew’s activities, manages resources, and ensures mission objectives are met. His experience as a cosmonaut spans multiple missions. Interesting Fact: Kononenko has spent a cumulative total of over 700 days in space, making him one of the most seasoned astronauts. His leadership and expertise contribute to the ISS’s continuous operation.

Nikolai Chub

Role: Nikolai Chub, a cosmonaut on Expedition 69/70, collaborates with his crewmates to conduct experiments, maintain the station, and support scientific investigations. Interesting Fact: Chub’s journey to space began as a military pilot, and he later joined the Russian space program. His dedication to advancing our knowledge of space benefits humanity as a whole.
